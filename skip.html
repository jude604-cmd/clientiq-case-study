(01) — Overview

Making restaurant promotions a one-minute job.

Skip is Canada's biggest food-delivery platform, part of the global Just Eat Takeaway organization. Free delivery is one of its most powerful growth levers — but keeping those promotions up to date across thousands of restaurant locations was painfully slow.

The Bulk Update Tool lets Skip's account managers update free-delivery promotions for thousands of locations at once. My work centred on one question: how do we make a high-stakes, high-volume task fast, accurate, and trustworthy — so the people who rely on it actually reach for it first?

(03) — The problem

Free delivery is central to Skip's business model.

One of the things that keeps Skip in a leading market position is its competitive delivery fees — it constantly gives customers free deliveries.

Updating it, though, was painfully time-consuming.

It took an experienced account manager about 2 minutes to update free delivery for a single McDonald's location — and there were over 1,100 McDonald's locations on the platform. Putting them all on promotion tied up the entire team for 1–2 days, with about another day to take them back off.

(04) — The existing tool

A bulk tool already existed — but it wasn't the first choice.

A Bulk Update Tool had been built to simplify the free-delivery update process. Yet account managers kept defaulting to the slow, location-by-location route through the restaurant profile. The tool wasn't winning their trust — and that became the real design problem.

“The Bulk Update Tool wasn't account managers' first choice — so the time savings it promised never landed.” Problem framing — 2020
(05) — Strategic context

A first step toward a much bigger goal.

Reducing manual effort — FTE — is an ongoing business objective at Skip. The Bulk Update Tool was phase one of a long-term vision: a single, automated system for every large-number management scenario across the platform.

(06) — Approach

A six-stage, research-led optimization process.

  1. Knowledge AlignmentGet everyone on the same page about the tool, the teams and the constraints.
  2. DiscoveryInterview and observe account managers to learn how the work really happens.
  3. HypothesisTurn assumptions into ranked, testable bets about what would help.
  4. ValidationPut prototypes in front of users and let the evidence decide.
  5. DesignDesign the flows in detail, simplifying wherever the research allowed.
  6. Usability TestingConfirm the experience held up before handoff and launch.
(07) — Discovery

Learning how account managers actually work.

I aligned with two key user groups, then ran discovery interviews and live observation to understand their day-to-day reality — what they update, how often, and where the friction really bites.

Who we design for

Two teams own this work: the Restaurant Success team and the NPM (National Partnership Management) team. Their needs overlap but aren't identical — so I mapped both before designing.

Interview & observation

Watching the work happen surfaced the friction interviews alone would miss — where people hesitated, double-checked, and quietly fell back to the manual path.

  • A knowledge-alignment workshop set shared language across teams.
  • Discovery interviews uncovered each group's real update patterns.
  • Live observation showed where confidence — not capability — broke down.

What the research surfaced

Synthesis pointed to a clear pattern: users needed visibility and control throughout the update — not just at the end — to trust a bulk action with thousands of locations.

(08) — Framing

One question to design against.

“How might we create an intuitive experience that increases efficiency and accuracy when users update restaurants' promotions?”

Prioritizing the work

With the problem framed, I prioritized what to tackle first — balancing user impact against effort, so the team could ship meaningful improvements within an agile cadence.

(09) — Approach

A hypothesis-driven way to ride the uncertainty.

By introducing hypothesis-driven validation into my process, I could ride the uncertainty inherent in the work toward much more favourable odds — making bets explicit, ranking them, and testing the riskiest ones first.

  1. Identify assumptionsSurface them during sprint planning, out in the open.
  2. Reframe as hypothesesRewrite each assumption as something testable.
  3. Rank by importanceOrder them so the riskiest, highest-value bets go first.
  4. Create a prototypeBuild just enough to put the hypothesis in front of users.
  5. ValidateTest, learn, and let the result steer the next iteration.

The sprint's focus

Each sprint took a single question. This one: how might we align the tool's credibility with users' expectations? — targeted at the Restaurant Success and NPM teams.

Key research questions

  • What information do users need to see when they update locations?
  • How many locations do users update each time?
  • What modifications do they need to make?
(10) — First hypothesis

Give users visibility, and confidence follows.

We believe that
users need to review and modify restaurant selections throughout the entire update process.
So if we
give users visibility of both the update selection and the update result,
we will see
users who are conscious and confident about what to do next.

Update selection & result

I prototyped a flow that kept the selection and the resulting changes visible at every step — so a bulk action never felt like a leap of faith. Then I validated it with the Restaurant Success team.

(11) — The breakthrough

Simplifying the core feature: just drop the file.

From interviews, I discovered the name-search feature didn't save Restaurant Success as much effort as expected. I tested approaches to improve it — but the real insight was that search itself was only one way to solve the problem.

The insight

The users' real need was to build a list of restaurants with different names — and selecting them from multiple searches was just one route there. In interviews I noticed they already had every location they needed sitting in a CSV from the marketing team. So I designed a solution where users simply drop that CSV into the search area to generate the list.

The prototype demonstrated the file-drop feature to engineering and leadership, and was used to validate the experience with the Restaurant Success team.

(12) — Final delivery

The shipped Bulk Update Tool.

The delivered experience kept selection and results visible end to end, and made the file-drop the fastest path to a thousand-location update.

Designed to be trusted

Every screen answers the question that kept users on the manual path: what exactly am I about to change, and did it work? Selection, modification and result stay visible throughout — so a bulk action feels deliberate, not risky.

  • Drop a CSV to build the location list in seconds.
  • Review and modify the selection before committing.
  • See the result of the update, confirmed at a glance.
